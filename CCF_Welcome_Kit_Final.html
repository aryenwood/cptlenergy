Safety Guidelines
Non-negotiable. Read it, know it, execute it on every job from your very first day.
⚠
Florida Law — Call 811 Before Every Dig Florida Statute 556 requires calling 811 before any ground penetration — no exceptions. Underground utilities are invisible from the surface: gas, electric, fiber, water. The office coordinates this during permitting, but every crew member must verbally confirm it has been done before any post hole digger touches the ground. This is a legal requirement and a life-safety issue.
🥾

Required PPE

Steel-toed boots every day. Safety glasses for any power tool. Gloves for material handling and wire work. High-vis vest near roadways or active driveways. Not optional.

🌡

Heat & Weather

Central Florida summers are serious. Hydrate constantly. Know heat exhaustion signs and look out for your crew. Your crew lead calls weather delays — active lightning within 10 miles stops the job.

⚙

Equipment Safety

Inspect every tool before use. Report defects immediately. Never use damaged equipment. Augers and post diggers require two-person handling on initial ground resistance confirmation.

🚨

Incident Reporting

Any injury, near-miss, or property damage — even minor — goes to your crew lead immediately and is logged the same day. Never wait. Never assume it'll be fine. This protects you.

🐾
Property & Pet ProtocolConfirm all pets are secured before opening any gate. Protect landscaping — roots and beds are not job site obstacles. Leave every property cleaner than you found it. This is what our 5-star reviews are built on.

What Good Looks Like
Before you swing a tool, know the standard you're building toward. This is what earns 5 stars.
📷

The Perfect Photo Set

Every CCF job gets documented in Company Cam — no exceptions. A complete photo set looks like this:

  • Before: Wide shot of the full property line before any work begins. Include the house in frame for context.
  • Mid-install: Posts set and plumbed, panels staged. Shows alignment and spacing before final attachment.
  • After: Completed fence from multiple angles. Close-up of gate hardware. Wide shot showing the full run.
  • Clean site: Final photo of the yard — no debris, no tools left behind, landscaping undisturbed.
📏

Plumb Posts, Clean Lines

This is the foundation of every fence we build. What plumb, professional posts look like:

  • Every post checked with a level — front-to-back and side-to-side. No eyeballing.
  • Consistent depth per spec. On vinyl and aluminum, this means the concrete footing is below the frost line and squared.
  • String line across the full run. If it doesn't track perfectly straight, it gets fixed before panels go on.
  • Gate posts reinforced per manufacturer spec. No sagging gates — ever.
✅

The Clean Close-Out

Leaving a job site is as important as the install itself. A proper CCF close-out:

  • All concrete dust, post hole clay, and packaging removed from the property completely.
  • Landscaping inspected — mulch beds restored, no tire tracks on grass, no damaged sprinkler heads.
  • Gate tested for full open/close cycle. Hardware tight. Latch clicks clean.
  • Walk the full fence line one final time. If you see something, fix it before you leave.
🌟
The Standard Is the StandardEvery job site should look like the photos in our gallery. Not most of them — all of them. When a homeowner walks outside and sees their new fence for the first time, we want the reaction to be "wow." That's what earns the review, the referral, and the reputation that keeps your schedule full.

You've Got Questions
Everyone does. Here are the ones every new hire asks — with straight answers.
What do I do if a customer asks me to change the plan?

You don't make that call — and that's a good thing. If a homeowner asks for something different from the original scope (different height, extra gate, moved fence line), tell them: "Let me get my crew lead to talk through that with you." Then immediately bring your crew lead into the conversation. Changes affect the permit, the materials, and the warranty. The office handles scope changes so you're never on the hook for a miscommunication.

What if I'm running late?

Call your crew lead and the office before your scheduled start. Not a text — a call. Things happen, and a heads-up goes a long way. What's not okay is silence. Your crew literally cannot start certain jobs without you, so a 10-minute warning lets everyone adjust. Life happens; communication is what separates a bad morning from a write-up.

Who do I call if something breaks on site?

Crew lead first, always. If you hit a water line, nick a cable, break a piece of equipment, or damage customer property — stop what you're doing and tell your crew lead immediately. They'll call the office and we'll handle it. The worst thing you can do is try to fix it quietly. We have insurance and processes for a reason. Honesty is always the right move here.

Can I work overtime?

Overtime is available and paid per Florida labor law (time-and-a-half over 40 hours/week). It's not guaranteed every week — it depends on the job schedule. Your crew lead will let you know when OT is available. Don't stay late without confirming with your crew lead first. We want you to earn, and we also want to make sure the hours are tracked properly so you get paid correctly.

When do I get paid?

Pay schedule is either weekly or biweekly — confirmed at your onboarding. Direct deposit is the default and the fastest way to get paid. Make sure your Direct Deposit Authorization form (Section 02) is completed on Day 1. If there's ever a question about your check, go to the office — they'll sort it out the same day.

What should I wear?

Steel-toed boots — mandatory, every day, no exceptions. Beyond that: work pants or shorts you can move in, a shirt you don't mind getting dirty. CCF will provide high-vis vests for jobs near roads. No open-toed shoes, no flip flops, no sandals. Dress like you're building something — because you are.

What if I have a problem with someone on my crew?

Talk to your crew lead first. If the issue involves your crew lead, go directly to the office or to Luke. We take crew dynamics seriously because a bad work environment makes everything worse. You won't be punished for speaking up — this is a zero-tolerance workplace for harassment and a zero-tolerance team for drama. Handle it early, handle it directly, and we'll back you up.
